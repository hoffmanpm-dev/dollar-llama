Take the Risk. Don't Sink the Ship.

June 2026

There is a version of risk that lives on a casino floor. Bright lights, a big lever, and the small but real chance that one pull changes your life. Most people, when they hear the word risk, picture some version of that. A long shot. A swing for the fences. A bet where you either win big or lose it all.

That picture is why a lot of people think they are not risk takers. They are not interested in the slot machine, so they assume they are not built for risk. But the slot machine is not what taking risk well actually looks like. In fact, it is close to the opposite.

I work with families who take real risk for a living, and so does mine. Katie and I invest in rental properties, an asset class about as far from a casino floor as you can get and still be doing something that can lose money. The difference isn't whether risk exists. It's how that risk is shaped. Good risk-taking is not about courage or appetite. It is about structure. Here is how I think about it.

It Is Not a Slot Machine

Start with what to avoid. A slot machine offers a small chance of a huge payoff and a large chance of losing everything you put in. For some people that is genuinely fun, and if you want to spend a little money for the entertainment of it, fine. But do not confuse it with investing. The math is built against you. The house keeps a slice of every dollar that moves through the machine, and over enough pulls, that slice is the only thing that is certain.

Plenty of things dressed up as investments work the same way. The lottery ticket disguised as a hot stock tip. The startup with no revenue and a great story. The crypto coin your cousin swears is about to 10x. These can pay off, and once in a while someone you know hits one. But the odds are literally against you, and the structure guarantees that most people walking in lose. If the only way you win is for the long shot to come in, you are not taking risk. You are gambling. Those are different activities, and only one of them builds wealth over time.

Do Not Let the Downside Sink You

Here is the rule that matters most. Take risk where the upside is real but the downside does not ruin you, and where you can do something about the downside before it arrives.

Our rental properties are a good example. On paper, buying a house with a loan and renting it out is risky. A foundation can crack. A sewer line can collapse under the yard. A roof can fail. A tenant can stop paying. A fire can take the whole thing. Any one of those can turn a good deal into a bad one.

So before we buy, we go looking for the trouble on purpose. We inspect the structure, the major systems, the roof, and we scope the sewer lateral, because a bad lateral is a five-figure surprise hiding underground. We buy title insurance so a problem in the property's history does not become our problem. We fully insure each property against fire and the other disasters that would otherwise be catastrophic. And we take asset protection seriously, with the right insurance limits and the right legal structures, so that a problem at one property cannot reach across and take down everything else.

Add all of that up and the shape of the risk changes. If everything goes well, we earn a nice return. If it does not, we can usually sell somewhere near what we paid. We can certainly still lose money. But the ship does not sink. That is the whole game. You are not trying to eliminate risk, which is impossible. You are trying to make sure that no single bad outcome can take you out of the game entirely.

Take Risk Where You Have an Edge

The next question is whether you have any business taking a particular risk at all. Most people learn their hardest lessons in the first few rounds of anything new. That tuition gets paid one way or another. The way to keep the bill small is to take risk inside your competence, alongside people operating inside theirs.

Katie and I did not walk into real estate as tourists. Katie knows construction and knows how to manage a crew. She has worked in the industry her entire career, so when a contractor tells her a job will take three weeks and cost a certain amount, she knows whether that is true. I know how to analyze a deal and how to line up financing. Between the two of us, we had a real edge going in. And we still make mistakes. We have misjudged a rehab budget and been surprised by an appraisal. Nothing has sunk us yet, but the edge did not make us immune. It just kept the mistakes survivable.

This is the test to apply to anyone who asks you to put money into their venture. Do not start with the size of the potential return. Start with the person. What experience do they have that is actually relevant to this specific thing? A brilliant dentist opening a restaurant is starting from zero, no matter how smart the dentist is, and if you back him, so are you. Relevant, hands-on experience is the only real edge, and it has to belong to someone in the deal, either the operator or you. If you cannot identify who owns that edge, you are not investing in a business. You are buying a slot machine with a pitch deck.

Run the Numbers

Once a risk passes those filters, you actually have to do the work of estimating what it is worth. Most people skip this part and substitute a feeling. They imagine the good outcome, decide they like it, and stop there.

The better habit is to project a realistic base case first. Not the dream, not the disaster, but the outcome you actually expect if things go roughly as planned. Then build a better case and a worse case around it and look at the whole range. What happens if rents come in 10% under what you assumed? What if the rehab runs long? What if it all goes right? You are not trying to predict the future. You are trying to understand how a range of futures would treat you, and whether you could live through the bad end of that range.

The key word in all of this is reasonable. I see people err in both directions. Some make every assumption rosy, stacking best cases on top of best cases until the spreadsheet promises a fortune that was never realistic. That is how you talk yourself into a bad deal. But just as many people do the opposite. They assume everything goes wrong at once: rents crater, the roof fails, and the market turns, all in the same year. That kind of analysis feels responsible, and once in a while it does save you from a mistake. The more common result, though, is that you never invest in anything, because nothing survives a worst-case-on-every-line stress test. Both errors come from the same place, which is letting how you feel about risk bend the numbers. The numbers should be honest. Your assumptions should be the ones you would defend to a skeptical but fair-minded friend, not the ones that justify the answer you already wanted.

Then comes the step almost everyone forgets. Compare the result to your alternatives. A risky deal that might return 8% is not attractive when a boring index fund has historically done about 10% for none of the work and none of the worry. The question is never just "could this make money." The question is whether it pays you enough more than the easy option to justify the extra risk and effort. If it does not, the boring choice wins, and there is no shame in that.

Know Your Risk Tolerance

Risk is also personal, and the right amount of it is not the same for everyone. For some people, simply moving money out of a money market fund or bonds and into public stocks, and living with the ups and downs that come with it, is all the risk they want or need. That is a completely legitimate place to be. For others, putting money into a new business or a real estate development feels natural and even comfortable.

Neither one is right or wrong. What matters is being honest with yourself about which person you are, because the worst outcomes tend to come from people taking risk they cannot actually stomach. They hold on while it is calm and then sell at the bottom when it gets rough, locking in the loss at the worst possible moment. A smaller risk you can hold through is worth more than a bigger one you will abandon under pressure. Know your tolerance, and build a plan you can actually stick to when things get uncomfortable, because they will.

Know Your Goals

The last filter is the one that quietly settles most of the others. Risk is not a virtue on its own. It is a tool for getting somewhere, and if you are already on track to get where you want to go, you do not need to take chances that only add the possibility of getting knocked off course.

I have watched people who had already won keep playing as if they had not, reaching for returns they did not need and exposing themselves to losses that could have undone years of careful work. Risking the lifestyle you have already secured to chase a net worth number you do not need is not ambition. It is bad math. If your goals are funded and your plan is working, the right amount of additional risk may be very little. The point of all of this was never to take as much risk as possible. It was to take the right risk, in the right places, in service of a life you have actually defined. Take risk that moves you toward your goals. Leave the rest on the table.

That is the difference between the casino and the rental house. One is built so the house wins and you eventually lose. The other, done carefully, is built so that even when you are wrong, you get to keep playing. If you want to go deeper on matching your investing to your own goals rather than someone else's scoreboard, that is the heart of Training for Your Race.
