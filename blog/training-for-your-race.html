Training for the Race You're Actually In

March 2026

I've never been a runner, but I once dated someone who was. She trained for marathons with a discipline that fascinated me. Five runs a week, each with a specific purpose. Long runs on the weekend built endurance. Tempo runs taught her to sustain effort at the edge of her aerobic capacity. Interval sessions built speed. Every run fit into a periodized training plan that cycled through different phases across the year.

She would talk about the importance of knowing what race you were training for. A 5K runner doesn't train like a marathoner. A marathoner doesn't train like an ultramarathoner. The physiology is different, the pacing is different, the fueling strategy is different. Training for the wrong race is worse than not training at all—it's a waste of effort and, worse, it can lead to injury.

The Simple Years

In the early years of your financial life, the race is simple. You earn, you save, you compound. The strategy is straightforward because your constraints are straightforward: maximize earnings, keep expenses low, invest the difference.

A 25-year-old software engineer and a 65-year-old retiree shouldn't have the same financial strategy. The engineer has decades of compounding ahead. Volatility is a feature, not a bug. He can take risk. The retiree is spending down his assets. He needs stability, predictable income, and capital preservation. The right strategy for each is completely different.

The Game Changes

But somewhere in the middle, things shift. You're no longer just saving and compounding. You have a family. You own real estate. You've built a career. Taxes matter. You have options. Do you stay at your job or pursue something higher-risk with better upside? Do you buy a bigger house or keep renting? Do you take on leverage? Do you start a business?

The calculus of what's "optimal" becomes personal. A venture-backed startup founder and a tenured professor have completely different risk profiles, time horizons, and financial constraints, even if they both earn six figures. A lawyer with $3 million in investable assets and a 10-year horizon to retirement isn't racing the same race as a 30-year-old with $50,000 saved and 35 years until they stop working.

The Right Scoreboard

This is where most financial advice fails. It gives you the scoreboard for someone else's race.

"Build wealth like this billionaire." Except you don't have $1 million to deploy. You don't have industry expertise to source private deals. You don't have a 40-year track record. You're not racing his race.

"Get rich quick with options." Except you need that money in three years for a down payment on a house. You're not racing that race either.

The right question isn't "What's the optimal portfolio?" It's "What's the optimal portfolio for my constraints?" For your time horizon. For your income volatility. For the major purchases you have ahead. For your ability to stomach losses without panicking and selling at the bottom. For the tax situation you're actually in.

When Pushing Harder Is the Right Call

This doesn't mean being passive. My ex-girlfriend didn't just jog casually because she was training for a marathon. She pushed hard, strategically. She ran tempo runs at near-maximal effort. She did interval sessions that left her gasping. But she did those things deliberately, as part of a plan designed for her specific race.

Your financial strategy should work the same way. If your race is a 35-year marathon to retirement, and you're 30 years old, pushing harder on stock exposure makes sense. You have time to recover from drawdowns. Compounding is your advantage. Use it.

But if your race is 5 years to retirement, and you need that capital, pushing harder means something different. You might push on career earnings. You might optimize taxes aggressively. You might push on optimizing your housing situation. But you shouldn't push on taking stock market risk that could crater just before you need to tap the funds.

Invest for the Life You're Living

Here's the hard part: your race might be less glamorous than someone else's. You might not be training for an ultramarathon (early retirement, venture-scale wealth, generational riches). You might be training for the 10K you're running this October, which is fine. A 10K is still a race. It still matters. And being the best 10K runner you can be is more valuable than being a mediocre marathoner.

A middle-class family with two kids, a mortgage, and reliable income doesn't need to get rich. They need to stay employed, save consistently, avoid catastrophic mistakes, and let compounding work. That's a completely valid race. And the strategy that wins that race is boring: diversified index funds, regular contributions, patient holding, rebalancing annually.

Meanwhile, someone with a volatile income, a high risk tolerance, and a short time horizon might win their race with a completely different playbook. More concentration. More active management. More tactical moves. Different strokes for different races.

The magic happens when you stop comparing your race to someone else's. Stop trying to run a marathoner's strategy when you're training for a 5K. Stop waiting for the "right time" to get started because you think your race should look different. Stop second-guessing your pace because the person next to you is running faster.

Figure out what race you're actually in. Build your strategy around that. Train deliberately, push hard on the things that matter for your constraints, and let everything else go.
